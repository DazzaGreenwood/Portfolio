eConfidence in World Commerce

The Role of the Notary

 

Presented by: Daniel Greenwood

Lecturer, MIT School of Architecture and Planning

Director, MIT E-Commerce Architecture Program

 

 

The role of the notary in the modern economy is well established, but precarious nonetheless.  Eventually (once the economy nears the end of its march into the information age), laws requiring notarizations could be subject to review and repeal if other solutions are more optimal.  Alternatively, notarization laws could survive, but will be updated to reflect the then current business and procedural requirements for the underlying data or transactions. 

 

It will be necessary over the mid-term (perhaps 3-5 years) for notaries to establish their worth and value in the emerging new economy.  This will include the ability to articulate a value proposition to businesses and others who would use or rely upon notarizations.  Electronic notarization that solves business needs at competitive prices will be important.  In the meantime, it will be necessary to prepare a transition position, in which several competitive candidates for electronic notarization technology and processes are seeded and developed and a deep business analysis is conducted to determine the optimal market entry, price points and public education. 

 

Safe For Now, But Electronic Transactions and Virtual Presence is Coming

 

Notaries are well established because the system of laws requiring notarization remains in place, and the economy is still largely based in the industrial age – document centered and made up of the same professions, work flows and transaction types as it was 50 years ago.  Real estate deals look largely as they did many years ago.  Law offices and courts and wills and trusts and certificates all continue to play their role and work more or less as they always have.  This state of affairs will continue for years.  But the role of the notary is precarious because the information economy is at the same time fast emerging.  Business models and transaction types are developing that bear little resemblance to industrial modes.  Statutes, regulations and judicial decisions are following.  Certain aspects will continue – for example: guns, lawyers and money continue to hold primacy.  But the form of action looks different:

 

Guns: Warfare is conducted differently – ever more reliant on technology for combat and logistics, as well as our command, control, communications and intelligence systems.  Military and intelligence information of critical nature is transacted electronically.  The security and risk control methods developed to maintain the reliability and integrity of these systems is fast commercialized and of use in the global commercial economy.

 

Lawyers: The “integrated justice” trend is forcing ever more collaboration between law enforcement systems at the local, state and federal levels.  Technical standards are being developed at organizations like OASIS/LegalXML to bring electronic court filings, transcripts, contracts and many other legal documents into the new age.  Online dispute resolution services are providing competitive alternatives to the courts, and are conducted electronically.

 

Money: Financial services industry is in the midst of a process, technology and business restructuring of unprecedented scope and depth.  Mainstay institutions increasingly become primarily data driven enterprises with expendable physical shells.  The tragedy of September 11th demonstrated the speed and quality with which financial institutions were able to restart operations in new places as a result of the electronic nature of their businesses.  Radically new market entrants, like PayPal, are at the same time rendering the last physical vestiges of financial institutions ever less meaningful. 

 

The above examples demonstrate a nascent trend.  All virtual.  All the time.  Meanwhile, other transactions are all virtual some of the time, or partly virtual all of the time.  Still other data will continue in a physical document form for the foreseeable future.  But the underlying story is that the economy is changing, and the role of notaries will have to change as well. 

 

Trust and Risk in the New Economy: a Lesson For Notaries

 

In the new economy, assuring reliable, responsive, efficient and tailored transactions will be more important than ever.  Some have hypothesized that there will be a need for new types of businesses to bring “trust” to electronic transactions.  At first blush, this may seem to be an opening for the electronic notary.  This type of approach is commonly known as the “trusted third party” model.  The scheme of a public key infrastructure and a “certification authority” were put forward to create the model.  This approach has been, in the near decade in which it has been available, a near total non-starter.  Why is “trust” failing to sell? 

 

Some people look upon the role of a notarization as a way to build “trust”.  But this may be the wrong “problem statement”.  Trust is an elusive concept that is fundamentally subjective and impossible to assure at measurable and repeatable quantities.  The demand in the information economy will increasingly be for economic loss control, not for exotic schemes at building “trust”.  The name of the game is “risk management”.  And to the extent a market exists in which other tools, professions and approaches are more competitive solutions, then the notarization will wither and die on the vine.  To the extent notaries can adapt and position their services as genuinely valuable in the new world commercial system, then you will survive and prosper. 

 

The Demand-Side of the Notarization Equation: Learning to Listen to Your Market

 

How do you know if you are providing a service that is positioned to survive in the new word commercial system?  There are many forms of econometric analysis that can be applied to model the market for goods and services and make predictions about future performance, but I suggest a simple approach.  Ask the following question:

 

“If it were not for statutory and regulatory requirements for the presence of a notarization, how large would the market for these services be?

 

In other words, to what extent would businesses, professionals, consumers and others seek out and voluntarily require the notarization of a document?  This type of analysis is not immediately necessary in the way it is for an industry without a built-in government monopoly of demand.  For the immediate future, the demand for notarization will continue unabated largely because the legal source of requirements for notarization is stable.  However, as our society continues its momentous lurch into the information age, deep legal reforms will erupt.  The advent of the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act and the E-Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (both of which contain provisions for electronic notarization) are only there merest early examples of the coming law reform that will accompany new ways of working, learning and living.  Will the legally mandated demand for notarization survive as legislatures take a fresh look at the actual need for consumer protection and risk management generally?  If notaries are providing a genuinely valuable service, that is well understood and widely recognized as useful, then I think the result will be that notarization requirements will be maintained. 

 

And so, we come back to the economic market analysis as a measure of whether the market for notarizations is inherently sound and would survive absent artificial government mandates.  If notarization requirements were lifted, some would probably continue to avail themselves of this unique resource.  However, overall it is doubtful that a robust demand would continue.  As transaction costs and operating costs increasingly become the sole metric upon which competition is based, any measure that can reduce the number of steps in a process and eliminate additional people, cost and expense will be welcomed.  In fact, such stream lining will be demanded by business who require economic competitiveness in a global economy. 

 

Why would business and other economic players seek to eliminate notarization requirements?  In some part this may be because of the lack of understanding and appreciation by the public at large of the merits of notarization.  In larger part, it may be because of the availability of so many other mechanisms to manage the transaction and operational risks.  What is the relative place of notarization in the context of risk management tools?  This strategic question holds the key to the future course for notaries – whether that be bright or dismal.  Here are some questions that can shed more light on the answers:

 

“Who would be the natural competitors of notaries, if the public were given the discretion to choose alternatives at will?  What are the price-points at which notarizations are optimally set in that kind of competitive environment?  What market segment and share would go to the competitors?  What are the risks, opportunities, strengths and weaknesses in an open market?  What could be done today to create an economic demand for notarizations among buyers who have no legal requirement for a notary? 

 

Notarization in the Risk Management Arsenal

 

Once a competitive analysis is done, notaries will have a vision of their optimal position in the global market for services of the type they offer.  It is likely that such an analysis will result in a conclusion that continued and revised government requirements for notarization should exist.  In addition, the analysis should seek market-driven (rather than government required) opportunities for the use of notarizations.  One hot and growing area of demand is security and authorization.  In the arena of risk management, notarization can be seen as a factor – one among many – that can assist in the avoidance or mitigation of transaction and operations risk.  For example, the underlying concept of notarization can fill a gap in the risk-puzzle of e-commerce and e-government related to electronic authentication.  It would be commercially useful to have a low-cost notarization process whereby the identity of a user were attested by a notary as a precondition to access to a protected online resource (like a large commercial electronic bank account or other important financial transaction – especially at the account initialization phase when “know your customer” banking rules to avoid money laundering are at play).  Similarly, a low cost notarization process would be useful for government transactions requiring authentication at a higher level. 

 

Mapping the Law to Emerging Technology and Business Practices

 

As new technologies emerge to effectuate signatures, notarizations will have to keep pace.  One interesting advanced research project at MIT’s E-Commerce Architecture Program sought to demonstrate “gesture-based” signatures.  The use of voice-activated interfaces and applications has been long tested.  Still around the corner, however, are even simpler to operate input devices for computers that rely solely upon the body language of users.  In the MIT research project, a demo was attempted that allowed users to move a cursor, do limited typing, and to signify “yes” or “no” to a final electronic transaction.  The motions of a hand moving resulted in the cursor movement.  The “Yes” was signified by a “thumbs up” motion, and the “No” by a “thumbs down” motion, in this particular pilot.  Query – how would one approach the notarization of such a signature?

 

Below are some examples of how current legal notarization requirements track to potential advances in technical and business process approaches.

 

 

Legal Requirement

Technology/Process Options

Personal Appearance

Physical presence and use of electronic notarization tools for electronic records.  In this case, the notary may apply something like a digital signature or perhaps something like a stegonographic seal (like a digital watermark).  Alternatively, the simple input of a PIN or smart card may suffice, if the applicable system includes the appropriate business controls.

 

Virtual presence – perhaps including video, voice, biomarkers and a secure, authenticated communications channel between the notary and the signer.  Virtual presence is now being considered for many types of situations where the law requires the presence of a person (including public meetings, legislative or regulatory hearings, court appearances, local government meetings, etc.).  The same types of requirements (see and be seen, hear and be heard, authenticate users, support relevant business process, etc) may be applicable to presence for purposes of notarization.

 

An electronic notarization (whether in person or virtually overseen) would have to include all the relevant information (name, date of commission expiration, etc.) that would have been required from a traditional notarization stamp under UETA and ESIGN.

Identification of the Signer

Physical presence with electronic notarization of electronic record could include existing ID process, without change

 

Use of electronic authentication credentials could be leveraged in physical or virtual notarizations (biometric, smart card, PIN recognized by trusted party, etc.)

 

Other more basic risk management tools may be more appropriate and cost effective.  For example, a call-back to a telephone number associated with the signer is quite effective as an identification means. 

Acknowledgment by Signer of a particular signature, document and transaction

Ascertaining acknowledgement and awareness would certainly require a screening of some kind, such as asking questions and probing to assure a proper cognitive state.  Theoretically, auditable evidence of such screening could be created and saved digitally for later forensic analysis if electronic notarizations (especially when premised on virtual presence) were conducted.

Lack of Duress of the Signer  

The same sort of screening as with acknowledgement and awareness would be useful to test for duress.  In addition, the use of commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) medical diagnostics equipment will be in wide use in the future.  The biomarkers observable with such equipment (respiration, cardio-vascular, sweat, pupil dialation, etc.) can pick up lies or undue stress that can be linked to duress or other fundamental obstacles to voluntariness.  It would also make sense to assure a video feed, if that is all that is available, can show whether a threatening person is just off camera.
